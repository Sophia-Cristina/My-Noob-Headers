Signal Processing is treated as a distinct domain. While many signals are electronic in nature, this is not universally the case. Accordingly, signal-processing-related components are organized in this directory, whereas elements strictly related to electronics and electrical engineering are located in `ysxElec`.

The boundary between these domains is non-trivial. Certain mathematical formulations used in signal processing are also applicable to electrical engineering. To avoid duplication, shared utilities are centralized where appropriate. For example, functions relevant to both domains can be found in:
  * `ysxSignal/ysxSigElec.h`

Conversely, if a signal-processing system requires specialized electronic functionality, it may be necessary to reference components from the `ysxElec` directory.

In general, cross-directory dependencies follow a pragmatic model: signal-processing modules may rely on electronic utilities, just as electronic systems may incorporate signal abstractions (e.g., streams). However, such dependencies are situational rather than foundational.

Signal Processing is distinct from data structures. Although it may utilize data representations, its focus is on transformation and analysis of signals rather than on storage or structural organization. Data related components, such as containers, protocols, and formats; are located in the `ysxData` directory.

Similarly, Signal Processing is not equivalent to coding or language-related systems. While signals may carry encoded information, coding infrastructures such as parsers, compilers, and language tools; are conceptually separate and are organized under the `ysxCode` directory.

Although most signal-processing systems are implemented on computers, the domain itself is not inherently tied to computing. Computers are general-purpose systems capable of supporting multiple paradigms, and signal processing represents only one such application area.
Finally, Signal Processing is not directly concerned with the internal electronic architecture of computing systems (e.g., ROM, RAM, or processor design), even though such components may be involved in implementation. For these aspects, refer to the `ysxComputer` directory.
